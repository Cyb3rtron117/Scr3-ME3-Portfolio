Clear the Deep!

18/04/25 - 25/06/25

Unity Engine

Solo

Semester 1

About the game:

In this 2D game, the ocean has been littered with barrels. The player, a submarine, has to find all the barrels they can within 90s and bring it back to the boat to salvage.
Along the way, the player can encounter different dangers and some unusual barrels.

This game was developed at the start of my studies, and after it was handed in in June, I continued to improve the game and its mechanics up until recently
I was even able to show it off at my University's showcase event, and at a Game dev Meetup

My Contribution:

After having learnt the fundamentals of scripting in C#, we were tasked to create a mechanic for a game, and then develop the game further.
This game's main focus was a grabbing mechanic. The player would be able to move around the map to find barrels. These could then be picked up and moved around.

The first version of the mechanic worked, but was heavily flawed. The player could pick up two barrels at once if they were close enough, and would constantly teleport to the right spot, rather than being parented. When I returned to improve the game, the grabbing mechanic was rewritten with new knowledge that I learned from participating in a Game Jam earlier this year.

This fixed the barrel issue, and allowed me to improve the visuals of a grabbed item to better communicate the idea. The mechanic was also reduced from being spread across 3 different scripts to being contained in only 1.
The movement system was also rewritten two times; the first to adapt the input to Unity's new input system, and the second to optimise the script.
I also created a spawning mechanic for the barrels, allowing random barrels to spawn in different spots, with the mechanic being triggered when the barrel is collected. The player could also collect points, which would be displayed upon winning/losing

Deckloop

30/07/25 - 29/11/25

Unity Engine

2 Person Team

Semester 2

About the game:

In this 3D game, the player is trapped in a timeloop, forced to relive the same 60s.
Throughout the map there are different colour-coded doors, each with a corresponding keycard hidden somewhere. The player must find these items, and progress through the loops to break free of this loop.

This game has a big focus on speedrunning, and the mechanics reflect that. The player can sprint, and throw cards across gaps to open doors that would take too long to open manually. It is even possible to beat the level in 60s

The game was created by me and another classmate, and we had the whole semester to create it.

My Contribution:

For this game, I created a lot of the systems, such as picking up and throwing cards, and unlocking different doors. I also created the movement system and time loop mechanic.
My teammate helped to create the foundations for the card reader system and the different functions each would have, and I refined it further and fixed errors when they occured.
I was also the lead in level design and created the tutorials.
We wanted to achieve more with the game, however with the time constraints we were happy with the product.
We were able to show it off at our University's showcase event, and at a Game dev Meetup

BloodCraft

12/08/25 - 17/11/25

Unity Engine

Solo

Semester 2

About the game:

BloodCraft is a 2D crafting game with a unique twist, where the player has to reach the end of a level through challenging combat.
Along the way, enemies drop resources that you can use to craft materials to aid you in your journey. But, be careful to not craft too much, because each recipe takes some health too.

The game was centered around a mechanic that we had to create ourselves. I chose to create a crafting system that also takes the player's health into the recipe.
The assignments were mostly focused around creating the mechanic, and our final assignemnt assessed whether we could successfully create a game that incorporated this mechanic.

My Contribution:

I looked at a few other games to see how each of them handeled crafting in a different way.
I looked at ASTRONEER (System Era Softworks 2016), VOLCANOIDS (Volcanoid 2019), TERRATECH (Payload Studios 2018) and Minecraft (Mojang Studios 2009)
Minecraft (Mojang Studios 2009) inspired me the most, however, the other games helped brainstorming about unique twists I could add.

To craft, the player has to find a crafting station, and upon interacting with it, it brings up the player's inventory and crafting UI. The player can select from a bunch of items to craft, and each item shows the required materials below the catalogue
The crafting works with each item in the crafter containing a recipe with lists of items needed, what images to display, and how many items are needed.
These requirements then get sent to the crafter's required materials to display this information to the player, and to have a recipe to compare the players inventory to when deciding whether or not to craft an item.
The system was created using string comparison, which I later realised was not very efficient. By then, the whole system would have needed to be rebuilt to fix it, which I unfortunately did not have the time for.

I also designed an inventory system, so the player can store materials, and also added the ability to equip weapons, shields and armour, each affecting the player's and enemy's damage and damage reduction ability.
The combat system was also created to accomodate 3 different classes of weapons, and would switch between the correct damage and hitbox depending on the equipped weapon.
To make the characters move, I used a list of waypoints the characters can travel to, and used Unity's Agent components with an extension to make it 2D compatible to make them move towards it. This allowed me to set different priority levels for the characters so that they do not run into each other, and they could navigate around the map and corners if the navmesh was baked.

EXTINCTION

14/03/25 - 26/06/25

Unity Engine

Solo

Semester 1

About the game:

This game was created at the start of my studies, and was my first attempt at creating a 2d game in Unity.
In this game, dinosaurs have been discovered by scientists. These dinosaurs were studied and eventually, broke out and caused havoc around the world.
As a last-ditch effort, scientists created a time machine, and a robot specifically designed to kill the dinosaurs, and sent them back in time.

Now, a few million years in the past, their goal is to burn everything, and cause: Extinction!

My Contribution:

I designed everything in this game, save for the forest background and the audio.
I designed the 2d movement, and created a jetpack for the player to use to be able to make large jumps and cross gaps. The player could also shoot fireballs out of their hands, and also use the weapon as a flamethrower.
The flamethrower and the jetpack both used the same fuel resource, and the player would have to replenish the fuel by siphoning from other dead robots.
The ideal game would have the dead robots be the player's past failed attempts, however, I could not add it in time for submission.

The enemies in this game were not very advanced, but still had good logic. The dinosaurs could jump, switch directions randomly, and attack the player. They could also chase the player if they were in the enemy's view. The player could also escape the dinosaurs, and they would go back to patrolling.
The enemies occasionally get stuck, however, I would rather rewrite the whole system than fix the bug.

For this game specifically, I went out of my way to learn about Unity's rule tiles and their limitations. I wanted the level to be burnable, since the player's arsenal consited of only fire-based weaponry.
I then learned how to make different rule tiles connect from a tutorial by VinarkDev on YouTube, and set it up to work with the player's weapons.

History of Turn-Based Games

22/05/26 - 08/06/26

Unity Engine

4 Person Team

Year 2, Semester 1

About the game:

The original brief for this assignemnt was to look at 80 years of history for any game genre, and create a timeline from it.
Other groups did exactly that, and some prensented these in class as big presentations.
However, I wanted to create something more interactive than a static timeline and a presentation. So I wondered: "Why not make it a game?"
After convincing my 3 other classmates, we started creating the game that the Lecturer can play in-class.

In this game, the player fights enemies that loosely resemble characters or information from a game on the timeline. The fights are a pre-recorded animation with no player input, but it fits the spirit of turn-based combat
After defeating the enemy in turn-based combat, the player reads the "loot" that the enemy dropped, and gets the information of the game.
The game consists of 4 seasons, each covering the part of the timeline each group member collected information on, and the player travels between these seasons to progress.

My Contribution:

I created the game, since my group members had little experience with the Unity Game engine. It is worth noting that my group consisted of 2 First Year students.
I designed all the systems and scripts in the game, such as making the player move, interacting with enemies and so on.
One feature that I created for Quality-of-Life was a complete fast-travel system between scenes. I noticed that if the player were to backtrack to a previous scene, the scene would be completely reset, with the player having to fight the same enemies.
So, I created a system that keeps track of which enemies the player fought previously, and kept track of which side of the level the player should spawn on. This feature was unfortunately never tested by the Lecturer, but I felt that without it, the game felt incomplete.
